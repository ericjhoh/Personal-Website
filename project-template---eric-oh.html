Overview

As part of a collaborative team, I designed and prototyped The Tele, an innovative assistive device that empowers individuals with hemiplegia (paralysis on one side of the body) to independently change a baby’s diaper. Addressing the challenge of a task that traditionally requires two hands, The Tele provides a one-handed solution that is intuitive, portable, and safe. The final design includes telescoping sidebars with single-button height adjustment, fabric-covered elastic loops to support the baby’s legs, and a lightweight high-density polyethylene base with a washable changing pad. These features enable the user to lift and secure the baby’s legs while maintaining easy access to clean the back, effectively mimicking the functionality of a second arm.
‍
Key Contributions:

  • Conducted in-depth user research through interviews with physical therapists, observational studies, and user testing to understand the challenges faced by parents with hemiplegia.
  • Refined designs through iterative prototyping, evolving from a ramp-style concept to the telescoping sidebar design based on usability testing and feedback.
  • Developed CAD models, selected materials (high-density polyethylene and elastic loops), and constructed prototypes to ensure durability, safety, and portability.
  • Tested prototypes extensively, identifying pain points and implementing design improvements to enhance ease of use, structural stability, and comfort.

The project challenged me to balance engineering constraints with human-centered design principles. Creating a device that was lightweight, easy to set up, and operated entirely with one hand required innovative solutions, such as the incorporation of locking hinges, telescoping mechanisms, and modular, washable components. Additionally, the project required close collaboration with teammates, rehabilitation specialists, and physical therapists to ensure the final product met real-world needs. The Tele provides a safe and practical solution, enabling caregivers with hemiplegia to change diapers independently. This project not only enhanced my skills in mechanical design, materials selection, CAD modeling, and user testing but also deepened my commitment to creating inclusive, impactful designs. Future iterations will focus on improving manufacturability and portability through injection-molded components and foldable designs.
